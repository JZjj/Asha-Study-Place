Distillation, Low-Rank Decomposition, Neural Architecture Search & Dynamic Computing

Four orthogonal ways to shrink or speed up a network that don't touch the weights' precision: transfer knowledge into a smaller student, factor big weight matrices into thin ones, let an algorithm design the architecture, and spend compute only where the input demands it.

NYU SAI Lab Spring 2026 KD · NAS · SVD · dynamic teacher/student · supernets early exit
01

Knowledge distillation

Transfer knowledge from a large, complex model (or ensemble) into a single smaller model that can actually be deployed under real-world constraints. The student is smaller and need not share the teacher's architecture.

The core setup Hinton 2015

A pretrained teacher DNN produces soft labels — a full probability distribution over classes, e.g. [0.2, 0.1, …, 0.1] — rather than a one-hot hard label. The student is trained to match those soft labels. Only the student's weights are updated; the teacher can be pretrained or trained jointly.

Why soft labels beat hard labels

A soft label carries more information than a one-hot target: it reveals the class relationships hidden in the hard label (a cat image scores some mass on "dog," none on "truck"). This "dark knowledge" improves the student's generalization and is what makes model compression without major accuracy loss possible.

Response-based distillation soft targets

The student's loss combines two terms — the distillation loss (student vs. teacher soft labels) and the ordinary cross-entropy loss (student vs. hard labels):

ℒtotal = c · ℒCE(hard) + (1−c) · ℒdistill(soft)c ≈ 0.9

Soft labels are produced by a temperature-scaled softmax over the logits zi:

p(zi, T) = exp(zi/T) ∕ Σj exp(zj/T)T = temperature
What temperature does

T = 1 is the normal softmax. Raising T > 1 softens the distribution — it flattens the peak so the small "wrong-class" probabilities grow, exposing more of the teacher's dark knowledge for the student to imitate. zi are the logits, p is the resulting soft label.

KL divergence — the distillation objective

The distillation loss is usually a Kullback–Leibler divergence, which measures how much a model distribution Q differs from a true distribution P (both discrete):

DKL(P ‖ Q) = Σx∈𝒳 P(x) · log( P(x) ∕ Q(x) )≥ 0, = 0 iff P = Q

Applied to the student's prediction against the teacher's target:

ℒ(ytrue, ypred) = ytrue · log(ytrue ∕ ypred) = ytrue · (log ytrue − log ypred)KL form

If P(x) = Q(x) for all x the divergence is exactly 0; otherwise it is strictly positive.

How smart does the teacher need to be? Cho & Hariharan 2019

  • Larger teachers are more accurate on their own, but that does not make them better teachers — a too-large teacher can even hurt the student.
  • As the teacher grows in capacity, the student can't find a solution in its own space that emulates the teacher well (shown across increasing depth and width of WRN/DenseNet teachers).
Fix: early-stop the teacher

Grid-searching the ideal teacher architecture is too expensive. Instead, early stop (ES) the teacher's training — a slightly under-trained teacher produces softer, more imitable targets and consistently lowers student error vs. a fully-trained ("Full") teacher.

Teacher Assistant (TAKD) Mirzadeh 2020

When the size gap between teacher and student is large, distillation breaks down. TAKD inserts intermediate-size TA networks to bridge it: the TA is first distilled from the teacher, then the TA plays teacher and distills into the student.

02

Distillation variants: where the knowledge comes from

Response-based KD only uses the last layer's outputs. Richer schemes tap intermediate features, let peers teach each other, or let a model teach itself.

Feature-based distillation FitNets, Romero 2014

Response-based knowledge fails to convey the teacher's intermediate-level supervision. Feature-based KD extracts intermediate feature maps from the teacher and uses them to guide the student — teaching it how the teacher processes information, which improves generalization and speeds convergence.

FitNets ("hints for thin deep nets") picks a hint layer in the teacher and a guided layer in the student, then minimizes their L2 distance. A regressor r(·) — itself conv layers — reshapes the student's feature map to the teacher's dimension:

ℒHT(WGuided, Wr) = ½ · ‖ uh(x; WHint) − r(vg(x; WGuided); Wr) ‖²hint-based training

Online distillation — deep mutual learning Zhang 2018

No fixed teacher: an ensemble of students learns collaboratively and teaches each other throughout training. Each network minimizes its own cross-entropy plus a KL term pulling it toward the other's predictions:

ℒΘ₁ = ℒC₁ + DKL(p2 ‖ p1) , ℒΘ₂ = ℒC₂ + DKL(p1 ‖ p2)mutual KL

The two models start from different initial conditions and update alternately per mini-batch; the idea extends to a whole cohort of students learning mutually.

Self-distillation "Be your own teacher," Zhang 2019

Split the network into blocks and condense knowledge from the deeper parts into the shallower ones — no separate teacher needed. Deep blocks supply distillation targets for shallow blocks alongside the hard-label cross-entropy.

Why it works

A model teaching itself with its own softened outputs encourages smoother decision boundaries and better internal consistency. It refines representations, reduces overfitting, and extracts richer knowledge from its own predictions.

Multi-teacher & cross-modal distillation

  • Multi-teacher (You 2017): combine several teachers not just by averaging their softened outputs (dark knowledge) but also in intermediate layers, by imposing a constraint on the relative dissimilarity among examples. Teachers may differ in architecture or just in weights.
  • Cross-modal: when labeled data for a new task is scarce (e.g. segmentation), pass unlabeled data to a teacher pretrained on a related task (e.g. classification) and use feature-based distillation. A teacher trained on a different vision task still understands visual inputs well enough to guide the student.

Distillation for quantization & pruning

Distillation isn't standalone — it can streamline the finetuning / retraining loop inside iterative pruning (prune part of the weights → retrain → repeat until the target sparsity is met) and inside quantization-aware training (quantize → train → repeat until convergence).

Distillation for language models Distilling step-by-step, Hsieh 2023

Given unlabeled data, an LLM can provide both a ground-truth label and a rationale (feature). Distilling step-by-step trains much smaller models with less training data for both finetuning and distillation.

  • Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei 2022): prompt the model to decompose a multi-step problem into intermediate steps, so more computation is allocated to harder problems. This is the mechanism that produces the rationales.
  • Use few-shot CoT prompting — an example rationale + an example label — to elicit a rationale on each new input; then train the small model on both the label and the rationale.
ℒ = ℒlabel + λ · ℒrationalerationale-augmented loss
Why the rationale helps

The rationale-generation loss forces the small model to learn the intermediate reasoning steps behind a prediction, which in turn guides it toward the correct final label — richer supervision than the bare label alone.

Relation between student and teacher

The student should share some architectural similarity with the teacher. It can be a mini version (shallower, thinner), a pruned version, or a quantized version of the teacher — tying this lecture back to Lectures 04 and 05.

03

Neural architecture search (NAS)

Stop hand-designing networks. NAS automates architecture design, using algorithms to explore the vast space of possible networks and find the most efficient, effective design for a task — replacing slow, suboptimal human experimentation.

The three components

Every NAS method decomposes into a loop between three parts. The scaling knobs of the search space are the usual suspects — width (#channels), depth (#layers), resolution (H×W), and their compound combination.

ComponentRole
Search space 𝒜The set of candidate architectures, fixed by an encoding method
Search strategyProposes an architecture a ∈ 𝒜 to try
Performance estimationReturns a performance estimate of a, feeding back into the strategy

RL-based NAS Zoph & Le 2016 · NASNet 2018

An RNN controller generates the model description of a network; it is trained with reinforcement learning to maximize the expected validation accuracy of the architectures it generates.

  • Loop: sample architecture A with probability p → train the child network to convergence → get validation accuracy R → scale the gradient of p by R to update the controller.
  • The controller predicts, per layer: number of filters, filter height/width, stride height/width. The action at each step depends on all previous actions (it is recurrent). Reward = validation-set accuracy.
  • NASNet transfers the searched cell to larger images: NASNet-A (6 @ 768) reaches 3.41% CIFAR error (2.65% + cutout), competitive with far larger hand-designed nets.
The cost problem

RL-NAS trains a full child network to convergence for every sampled architecture — enormously expensive. The next three methods are all attempts to avoid that.

DARTS — differentiable architecture search Liu 2018

Instead of discrete search, place a mixture of candidate operations on each edge and take a softmax-weighted sum, making the whole search space continuous and differentiable. With three candidate convs:

si = exp(ai) ∕ Σj exp(aj) , y = s1y1 + s2y2 + s3y3softmax over ops
ō(i,j)(x) = Σo∈𝒪 [ exp(αo(i,j)) ∕ Σo′ exp(αo′(i,j)) ] · o(x)mixed edge

Training is a bilevel optimization: architecture weights α on validation, network weights w on train.

minα ℒval(w*(α), α) s.t. w*(α) = argminw ℒtrain(w, α)bilevel

At the end, simply keep the operation with the highest s on each edge; an extra regularization term can push s toward one-hot.

ProxylessNAS Cai 2018

Earlier methods search on a small proxy task then transfer. ProxylessNAS searches directly on the target task and target hardware. It keeps DARTS-style architecture parameters but adds binary gates (0 = prune, 1 = keep) so only one path sits in memory at a time — solving DARTS's memory blow-up. Weight params and architecture params are updated in alternating steps.

Crucially it makes latency differentiable and part of the loss, so the search is hardware-aware:

𝔼[latency] = Σi 𝔼[latencyi] , where 𝔼[latencyi] = α·F(conv3×3) + β·F(conv5×5) + … + ζ·F(pool3×3)expected latency
Loss = LossCE + λ1‖w‖₂² + λ2 · 𝔼[latency]accuracy + latency

Once-For-All (OFA) Cai 2019

Train one supernet that contains many smaller subnetworks — all trained jointly, with small subnets nested inside larger ones. Each forward pass samples a subnetwork and trains its weights. Once trained, you can directly deploy any subnet to Cloud / Mobile / Tiny-AI targets with no retraining.

  • To pick a subnet, OFA builds neural-network-twins — a search DNN that takes an architecture as input and predicts its latency and accuracy, returning the subnet meeting the deployment criteria.
  • Twins are fit by sampling 16K subnetworks at varied architectures and input sizes and measuring accuracy on 10K held-out validation images.
  • With progressive shrinking (PS), OFALarge hits 80.0% ImageNet top-1 at 595M MACs — a claimed 14× MAC reduction vs. comparable models; OFA w/ PS reaches 76.0% at just 230M MACs.
The payoff

OFA amortizes search: train the supernet once, then specialize for every deployment target for free — instead of re-running an expensive search per target — greatly cutting total training cost.

04

Low-rank factorization

A big weight matrix often has redundant structure. Replace one m×n matrix with the product of two thin ones — and train the thin factors directly.

The decomposition

Factor a weight matrix W (m×n) into W1 (m×r) and W2 (r×n) with the inner rank r ≤ min(m, n):

W = W1 · W2 , W1 ∈ ℝm×r, W2 ∈ ℝr×ntrain W₁, W₂ instead of W

You store and train the two small factors instead of W — less storage, fewer parameters, provided r is small enough.

SVD as the tool closed form

Singular Value Decomposition writes W = U · R · VT, which folds into the two-factor form:

  • V (n×r): orthonormal eigenvectors of WTW.
  • U (m×r): orthonormal eigenvectors of WWT.
  • R: the r×r diagonal singular-value matrix, where r is the rank of W.
Decomposition alone doesn't save anything

Parameters go from mn to mr + rn = r(m+n). At full rank r = min(m,n), giving min(m,n)(m+n) ≥ mn — the parameter count increases. The saving comes only from rank truncation.

Compute cost tells the same story

For an input X (s×m), the dense matmul XW costs smn. Factored, it becomes two matmuls:

cost(XW1W2) = smr + snr = s(m+n)r = s(m+n)·min(m,n)vs. dense smn

Again, without truncating r the computational cost rises — you must throw away small singular values to win.

Rank truncation — worked example

Take A = [[3,2],[2,3],[1,0]]. SVD gives singular values σ₁ = 5.24, σ₂ = 1.31. Truncate the smaller one and reconstruct from the top singular triplet:

A1 = σ1 u1 v1T ≈ [[2.9, 2.7], [2.7, 2.9], [1.3, 1.2]]rank-1 approximation

The approximation is close to the original while dropping half the singular structure — cutting compute and storage. Because a raw truncated SVD is only optimal in Frobenius norm, we can do better by finetuning the factors on real data:

minW₁,W₂ ΣX∈𝒟 ‖ XW − XW1W2 ‖²local finetuning

QSVD & other decompositions

  • QSVD (Wang 2025, NeurIPS): a joint SVD + quantization method for vision-language models — (1) joint SVD over the Q/K/V weights, (2) a learnable rank allocation per component, (3) post-training quantization with outlier smoothing — compressing QKV in low-precision VLMs.
  • CP decomposition (Lebedev 2014) and Tucker decomposition (Kim 2015) generalize the idea to conv tensors; each trades computational efficiency against accuracy differently.
05

Dynamic / conditional computation

Don't run the whole network on every input. Activate only the parts you need, chosen at run time from the input itself — spending compute where the sample is hard and skipping it where the sample is easy.

Dynamic channel pruning Feature boosting & suppression, Gao 2018

A lightweight channel saliency predictor generates a channel-wise importance measure per input. It subsamples the previous feature map, predicts saliencies gl(x), applies a winners-take-all gate πl(x) to keep only the top channels, and multiplies them back in (with ReLU) — boosting important channels and suppressing the rest without permanently deleting them.

SkipNet & dynamic gating Wang 2018

SkipNet is a modified residual network with a gating network that selectively skips whole conv blocks based on the activations of the previous layer. Easy inputs skip many blocks; hard inputs use more.

Grelax(x) = 𝕀(S(x) ≥ 0.5) on the forward pass ; S(x) on the backward passgate w/ STE
Why two definitions of the gate

The forward pass needs a hard binary decision (run the block or not), but a step function has zero gradient. So the backward pass uses the straight-through estimator (STE) — it substitutes the smooth S(x) so gradients can flow and the gating network is trainable.

Early exit — BranchyNet Teerapittayanon 2016

Data samples are not equal in difficulty (CIFAR10-Easy vs. CIFAR10-Hard). Easy samples only need a few layers to be classified correctly, so BranchyNet adds side exit branches along the backbone. At inference a confidence score is computed at each exit; if it clears a threshold, the output is produced locally and the rest of the network is skipped — faster inference.

entropy(y) = Σc∈𝒞 yc · log ycconfidence / exit criterion
The unifying intuition

Dynamic channel pruning, SkipNet gating, and BranchyNet early exit are three granularities of the same idea — allocate compute per input: skip channels, skip blocks, or skip the entire tail of the network once the answer is already confident.

★

Exam recap

KD in one line

Train a small student to match a big teacher's soft labels. Loss = c·CE + (1−c)·distill, c≈0.9.

Temperature

p(z,T)=softmax(z/T). Higher T softens the distribution, exposing dark knowledge. Distill loss is a KL divergence.

Teacher size

Bigger ≠ better teacher; too-large hurts. Fix with early stop or a teacher assistant (TAKD) to bridge the gap.

KD variants

Feature-based (FitNets hints), online (mutual learning), self-distillation, multi-teacher, cross-modal, LLM step-by-step.

NAS components

Search space 𝒜 → search strategy → performance estimation, in a loop.

NAS methods

RL controller (expensive) → DARTS (differentiable, softmax edges) → ProxylessNAS (binary gates + latency) → OFA (one supernet).

Low-rank / SVD

W=W₁W₂. Only helps with rank truncation (drop small σ). Cost s(m+n)r; finetune factors on data.

Dynamic compute

Spend compute per input: dynamic channels (FBS), skip blocks (SkipNet+STE), early exit (BranchyNet, entropy threshold).
