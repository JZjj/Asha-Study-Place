Efficiency Strategies for Large Language Models

Why LLM inference is memory-bound, where the weird outliers in activations come from, and the four families of fixes — quantization (with outlier smoothing), pruning, KV-cache tricks, and low-rank decomposition.

01

Autoregressive inference & the KV cache

Everything in this lecture exists to fight one fact: a decoder LLM generates one token at a time, re-using every past token's key/value on every step. That re-use is what the KV cache stores — and what dominates memory.

Generation is autoregressive GPT-style

A stack of decoder blocks (each = self-attention + FFN) predicts the next token from all previous tokens. The output is fed back in as the next input — round after round — until <EOS>. Because attention needs the key and value of every earlier token, we must buffer K and V rather than recompute them.

Why cache K and V

In block Q Kᵀ → softmax → ·V, the query is only the current token, but the keys/values span the whole history. Recomputing all past K/V every step would be quadratic redundant work, so they are saved once and reused — this buffer is the KV cache.

Two phases: prefill vs decode

PhaseWhat happensCompute character
PrefillThe entire prompt / context is processed jointly in one pass; every token's K/V is written into the cache.Large matmuls over all prompt tokens — compute-bound, parallel.
DecodeGenerate responses one token at a time; each round appends the new token's K/V and reads the whole cache back.One tiny query vs a growing cache — memory-bound, serial.

Notation from the slides: Ki,j is the key vector for the i-th token in the j-th layer, shape 1×E; Vi,j the matching value vector. The cache holds one K and one V per token, per layer.

KV cache ∝ 2 × nlayers × L × E × precisionK + V, per token, per layer
The bottleneck

The cache grows linearly with sequence length and never shrinks during a generation. Long contexts make decode dominated by reading the cache from memory, not by arithmetic — so cutting KV memory (quantize it, prune it, or share it) is the highest-leverage lever for decode speed.

02

Large-model data distribution: the outlier problem

You cannot quantize an LLM naively — its activations contain enormous outliers that blow up the quantization range. Understanding what kind of outlier and why it exists is the whole basis of LLM quantization.

First, why activation quantization is hard

  • Activation quantization must be dynamic — ranges depend on the input, so scales are computed at run time, adding compute overhead.
  • Activations then pass nonlinear functions that are very sensitive to quantization error, so you must dequantize back to FP16/32 around them (FP2INT → INT matmul → INT2FP → norm → ReLU → …).
  • Still worth it: for large models with heavy compute demand, even dynamically-quantized activations significantly reduce latency.

Granularity of quantization

Weights and activations can be quantized at different granularities, trading accuracy against hardware cost:

Tensor-based

One scale for the whole tensor. Cheapest hardware, highest quant error.

Vector-based

One scale per row/column vector. Middle ground.

Group-based

One scale per small group of elements. Lowest error, highest hardware cost.

The rule

Higher granularity → lower quantization error but higher hardware implementation cost. Finer scales isolate outliers into smaller groups, but each group needs its own scale/zero-point bookkeeping.

Two kinds of outlier the taxonomy

For an activation matrix A, the lecture defines two distinct outlier phenomena with concrete thresholds:

Massive: Aij > η·mean(|A|) and Aij > γe.g. η=300, γ=50
Channelwise: mean(Ai) > η·std(A)+mean(|A|) and std(Ai) < βe.g. η=3, β=0.6
  • Massive activation: a single element that is gigantic relative to the mean (can reach into the thousands). Sparse but extreme.
  • Channelwise outlier: an entire channel whose mean is high but whose spread is low — a whole column consistently offset.

Where they show up — CLIP and LLaMA

Profiling CLIP (Radford et al., 2021; image encoder ×24, fusion ×40) shows massive activations at intermediate positions x₁, y₁, y₅, while x₂ exhibits channelwise outliers that persist across layers. The same massive activations appear in LLaMA2-13B (Sun et al., "Massive Activations in LLMs," 2402.17762): a single spike in x₁ that dwarfs every other value.

Why massive activations exist · Sun et al. 2024

They are not random spikes or errors — they are learned, input-agnostic constants that act as implicit bias terms. The argument: LLMs use them to inject bias into the self-attention computation, letting certain tokens always attract disproportionately high attention (an attention "anchor"). The lecture notes no clear conclusion has been reached.

And they are load-bearing · Raman, Sharma & Zhang 2025

Truncating massive activations (setting them to the mean or to zero at y₇) causes catastrophic perplexity blow-up — e.g. LLaMA2-13B WikiText goes from 4.36 → ~1.3 million. The distortion is real, but you cannot simply delete them. Massive activations also occur in other attention-based foundation models.

How a massive activation propagates Llama-2 7B

  • y₇ of layer 3 first produces the massive activation (MA), inside the first text token; it first appears in y₆ of token 0.
  • In the following layers, x₁ and y₁ carry the MA — magnitudes that can reach the thousands (top-magnitude of x₁ jumps to ~1400 from layer 4 onward).
  • The MA then propagates through the residual link across layers, which is why it appears everywhere downstream.
  • Nuance: truncating the MA that is carried by the residual connection does not cause a large accuracy drop — it is the originating MA that matters.

How channelwise outliers form

The layer-normalization pipeline itself manufactures channelwise outliers in two steps:

Step 1 — standardize: X′ = (Xr − μr) / σr for each r∈Lper-token normalize
Step 2 — scale + bias: Ye = αeX′e + βe for each e∈Eper-channel affine
  • x₁ already contains some channelwise outliers, but not significant ones.
  • Standardization amplifies them: when a token's standard deviation σr is low, dividing by it makes channel outliers far more pronounced.
  • The scale (α) and bias (β) factors of the norm layer add still more — profiling CLIP's ln_weight/ln_bias shows spiky per-channel values.
  • Root cause of the pre-existing outliers in x₁: the output projection Wout produces minor channelwise outliers in y₅, and the residual link carries those forward from preceding layers.
The strategy this motivates

Standard LLM post-training quantization is two stages: ① outlier smoothing (redistribute the difficulty) then ② post-training quantization. Sections 3–4 are exactly these two stages.

03

Quantization stage 1: outlier smoothing

If activations have outliers and weights don't, move the difficulty into the weights — or rotate/permute it away — using a transformation that leaves the math unchanged. Every method here is a mathematically-equivalent rewrite of Y = AW.

SmoothQuant migrate difficulty

Xiao et al. (PMLR 2023). Divide the activation by a per-channel factor s and multiply the matching weight row by the same s — the product is identical, but now both are easy to quantize.

Y = (X·diag(s)⁻¹)·(diag(s)·W) = X̂ Ŵequivalent transform
sj = max(|Xj|)α / max(|Wj|)1−αper-channel smoothing factor
Reading the knob

α is a hyperparameter tuned on a calibration dataset. It slides difficulty between activations (large α) and weights (small α). The i-th column of X pairs with the i-th row of W, so the rescale is exact.

AWQ — Activation-aware Weight Quantization

Lin et al. (MLSys 2024). Same shape as SmoothQuant, but instead of a hand-set formula it makes the scale learnable, minimizing the actual output error:

s* = argmins ℒ(s), ℒ(s) = ‖Q(W·diag(s))(diag(s)⁻¹X) − WX‖learn the scale

Across LLaMA / Llama-2 at INT3-g128 and INT4-g128, AWQ beats round-to-nearest and GPTQ on WikiText perplexity — e.g. Llama-2-7B INT4-g128 reaches 5.60 vs FP16's 5.47.

QuaRot — rotate the outliers away orthogonal R

Ashkboos et al. (2404.00456). Insert an orthogonal matrix R (RᵀR = RRᵀ = I) between activation and weight. It cancels out mathematically but spreads the outliers so both AR and RᵀW quantize cleanly:

(AR)·(RᵀW) = AW ⇒ Q(AR)Q(RᵀW)outlier-free 4-bit
  • Offline vs online: RᵀW is folded into the weights offline; AR is absorbed by modifying the previous layer's weight matrix — almost no extra inference cost.
  • The catch with nonlinearities: LN(AWR) ≠ LN(AW)×R. When a nonlinear function (norm, RoPE, softmax) follows the linear layer, the rotation must be applied online.
  • Fix: use a Hadamard matrix (entries only +1/−1) so the online rotation is just adds/subtracts — cheap enough to run dynamically around RoPE and the KV cache.

DuQuant & SpinQuant — more transforms

MethodTransformIdea
DuQuant
Lin et al. NeurIPS 2024		Permutation P (PᵀP = PPᵀ = I): (AP)(PᵀW)=AWA permutation also leaves the output unchanged but redistributes outliers across quantization groups. DuQuant combines scalar-, rotation-, and permutation-based invariances to mitigate outliers.

The through-line

Scaling (SmoothQuant/AWQ), rotation (QuaRot/SpinQuant), and permutation (DuQuant) are all invariance tricks: apply T to the activation and T⁻¹ to the weight so Y is unchanged, but the outliers are flattened before quantization.

04

Quantization stage 2: fitting the quantized parameters

Once outliers are smoothed, you still have to choose the quantized weights/scales. Use a small calibration dataset and optimize each layer to reproduce its own outputs — the only question is whether you do it in parallel or in sequence.

Parallel vs sequential update

Parallel updateSequential update
Calibration dataProfile xc, yc,1, yc,2, yc,3 once from the full-precision model.Profile only the first layer, quantize it, then regenerate the input for the next layer from the already-quantized previous layer.
Objective (layer ℓ)minθℓ Σ ‖yc,ℓ − Fℓθℓ(xc,ℓ)‖²
Input to layer ℓOriginal full-precision activation.xc,ℓ = Fℓ−1θ*(xc,ℓ−1) — uses the quantized layer's real output.
Trade-offSimple, all layers at once; ignores compounding error.Accounts for accumulated quantization error down the stack; must be done in order to the final layer.
Why sequential wins on accuracy

Each quantized layer perturbs its output slightly. Sequential update feeds that perturbed output into the next layer's calibration, so later layers compensate for earlier errors instead of assuming perfect inputs.

AdaQuant learn scale + correction

Hubara et al. (PMLR 2021). Jointly optimizes the weight scale Δw, activation scale Δx, and a weight correction V against the layer's FP output, on a small calibration set:

(Δ̂w, Δ̂x, V̂) = argmin ‖WX − QΔw(W′)QΔx(X)‖²AdaQuant

A Sequential AdaQuant variant threads each layer's quantized output into the next, with Xq = σ(QΔw(Wℓ−1+Vℓ−1)·QΔx(Xqℓ−1)).

Clustering instead of quantization — LUT inference

Uniform quantization snaps values to a fixed grid, which handles outliers badly. Clustering (K-means on activations and weights) places centroids where the data actually is, so it tolerates outliers far better.

ValuesUniform quant → errorClustering → error
10.4 1.2 8.7 0.6 9.9 2.2 1.8 12.0snaps to grid → 1.3 (high)centroids 10.25 / 1.45 → 0.75 (low)
  • LUT-based inference: with a 4-bit input and K weight centroids, every possible (input-centroid × weight-centroid) product is precomputed into a lookup table — multiply/add become table reads.
  • CodeQuant (Yin et al., ICLR 2026) unifies clustering + quantization for low-precision Mixture-of-Experts: ① activation-oriented outlier smoothing, ② permutation-invariant outlier grouping, ③ adaptive weight clustering + centroid finetuning, ④ LUT-based kernel/system design.
Key insight

Compared to quantization, the clustering operation exhibits greater tolerance to outliers — centroids can sit on an extreme value instead of stretching a whole uniform grid to reach it.

05

Pruning on large models

Two pruning fronts on an LLM: prune the weights (Wanda) with input awareness, and prune the KV cache — the memory that actually chokes decoding.

Weight pruning: from magnitude to Wanda

Plain magnitude pruning zeros the smallest weights, but its major drawback is that it ignores the input — a small weight that always multiplies a large activation still matters. Wanda (Sun et al., 2306.11695) fixes this by scoring each weight with the input's norm:

Magnitude: S = |W| Wanda: S = |W| · ‖X‖₂importance score
  • If a weight's corresponding input magnitude is large, its output is large → keep it. Scores are grouped per output.
  • Needs a few calibration samples to estimate ‖X‖₂, but introduces no weight update.
  • On WikiText, Wanda matches SparseGPT (which does update weights) at 50%, 4:8, and 2:4 sparsity — e.g. Llama-2-7B 50%: Wanda 6.42 vs SparseGPT 6.51, no retraining.

KV-cache pruning attacks decode memory

The KV cache is the decode bottleneck (§1), so drop the entries that don't matter. Ge et al. ("Model tells you what to discard," 2310.01801) profile the attention score of each token and find:

  • Different attention heads weight KV vectors very differently (some care about special tokens, some about locality, some about punctuation, some are diffuse).
  • KV importance also varies across layers — so the pruning policy should be adaptive per head and per layer, not global.

StreamingLLM & the attention sink

Xiao et al. (2309.17453). Naive window attention (keep only the last L tokens' KV) collapses in quality. The surprising fix:

Attention sink

Simply keeping the KV of the first few tokens — alongside the sliding window — largely recovers full performance. LLMs place strong attention on initial tokens as a "sink," even when those tokens are not semantically important (they soak up leftover softmax mass). StreamingLLM = a handful of sink tokens + a rolling window, giving bounded KV memory for effectively infinite streams.

The progression: (a) dense (full cache) → (b) window (evict old, quality drops) → (c) sliding window w/ recomputation (expensive) → (d) StreamingLLM (sink + window, cheap and stable).

06

Low-rank decomposition for LLMs

A big weight matrix is often approximately low-rank. Factor W ≈ W₁W₂ and you shrink parameters, MACs, and — done right in attention — the KV cache itself.

The basic factorization

Replace an m×n weight W by two thin matrices W₁ (m×r) and W₂ (r×n) with rank r ≤ min(m,n). Train W₁, W₂ directly instead of W.

Wm×n = W₁m×r · W₂r×nlow-rank form
params: mn → r(m+n) · compute: smn → s(m+n)rsavings need r small
Only helps with rank truncation

Full SVD gives W = U R Vᵀ where V holds eigenvectors of WᵀW, U of WWᵀ, and R is the diagonal singular-value matrix (rank r). If you keep all singular values, r = min(m,n) and both parameter count and compute increase. The win comes only from truncating small singular values.

QSVD — low-rank Q/K/V with a shared latent NeurIPS 2025

Wang, Wang & Zhang, "QSVD." Attention has three big projections Wq, Wk, Wv (each E×E). SVD each into down/up factors, then go further by concatenating and sharing the down-projection.

SchemeTotal MACsWeight paramsKV cache size
Original (dense Q/K/V)3LE²3E²2LE
Per-matrix SVD6LrE6rE2rL
QSVD (shared latent)4LrE4rErL

The trick is to concat and SVD together: [Wq,Wk,Wv] = Wconcat ≈ Wᵈ Σ Wᵘ = Wᵈqkv·[Wᵘq,Wᵘk,Wᵘv], splitting the singular values as Wᵈqkv=WᵈrΣr1/2. Now a single shared latent Cqkv = X·Wᵈqkv is cached, and K, V are reconstructed from it:

K = Cqkv Wᵘk, V = Cqkv Wᵘvcache the shared latent, not K and V
Why the KV cache halves

Instead of caching both K and V (2rL), you cache only the shared latent Cqkv (rL) and multiply by the small up-projections at read time. Same idea as Multi-head Latent Attention: store a compressed latent, expand on demand.

Assigning rank per layer

Not every layer deserves the same rank. Score a layer's sensitivity by how much the loss moves with its singular values, then allocate a global rank budget R proportionally:

importance = Σi 𝔼D( |dL/dσi| )from (L(σ+Δσ)−L(σ))/Δσ ≈ dL/dσ

On SmolVLM-2B, LLaVA-Next-7B and LLaVA-v1.5-13B (ScienceQA-IMG), QSVD reaches comparable or better accuracy than the original model at reduced hardware cost.

The problem SVD ignores → WSVD ICLR 2026

Plain SVD minimizes argminW′‖W − W′‖² with W′=DU — but it treats every weight element as equally important, which is false. WSVD (Wang et al.) weights each element by its activation:

argminW′ Σij ‖aijWij − aijW′ij‖weighted low-rank
No closed form → learn it

Unlike plain SVD, the weighted objective has no closed-form solution, so D and U are trained with gradient descent. Element importance is estimated by a first-order expansion L(w) − L(w′) ≈ (dL/dw)(w − w′) — the same Taylor-importance idea used in pruning, now steering the factorization toward the weights that actually affect the loss.

★

Exam recap

Prefill vs decode

Prefill = whole prompt at once, compute-bound. Decode = one token/step, memory-bound, reads the growing KV cache.

KV cache

Stores Ki,j, Vi,j per token per layer. Size ∝ 2·nlayers·L·E·precision — grows linearly, dominates decode memory.

Two outliers

Massive: one huge element (A>η·mean, A>γ). Channelwise: whole channel, high mean / low std.

Massive activations

Learned input-agnostic bias terms feeding attention. Truncating them wrecks accuracy — but the residual-carried copies can be cut.

Smoothing = invariance

Scale (SmoothQuant/AWQ), rotate (QuaRot/SpinQuant), permute (DuQuant): apply T and T⁻¹ so Y=AW is unchanged but outliers flatten.

QuaRot detail

Orthogonal/Hadamard R, RᵀR=I. Offline when linear; must go online when a nonlinearity follows since LN(AWR)≠LN(AW)R.

Sequential > parallel

Calibrate each layer on the quantized previous layer's output → compensates for accumulated error. Clustering beats uniform quant on outliers.

Wanda

S = |W|·‖X‖₂ — input-aware pruning, per output, no weight update. Matches SparseGPT.

Attention sink

StreamingLLM keeps a few initial tokens' KV + a window. Initial tokens act as a "sink" for excess attention → bounded memory.

Low-rank only helps truncated

W=URVᵀ. Keep all singular values → params & compute grow. Savings require truncating small σ (r ≪ min(m,n)).

QSVD

Concat+SVD Q/K/V into a shared latent Cqkv. Cache the latent (rL) not K&V (2rL); reconstruct K=C·Wᵘk, V=C·Wᵘv.

WSVD

Weight the SVD error by activation |aij|. No closed form → learn D,U. Fixes SVD's "all elements equal" false assumption.
