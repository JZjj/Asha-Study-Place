Efficient DNN Training, PEFT & Speculative Decoding

Three ways to spend less: train the model more cheaply (less compute, less memory), adapt a giant model by touching only a sliver of its weights, and decode faster at inference by letting a small model guess ahead. The math of forward/backward passes ties it all together.

01

The anatomy of training: forward, backward, memory

Every efficiency trick in this lecture attacks one of three quantities: the compute of the two backward matmuls, the memory held to enable them, or the parameters that get updated. Start by knowing exactly what training does.

Forward pass of a linear layer

A fully-connected layer during forward propagation is just a matrix multiply. With batch B, input channels Cin, output channels Cout:

Y = X · W(B×Cin) · (Cin×Cout) = (B×Cout)

A convolution collapses to the same shape once you im2col it. Assuming a 1×1 kernel, the matrix view of a conv layer is X(BHW×C) · W(C×N) = Y(BHW×N) — the batch-and-spatial dimensions are flattened into the rows.

Backward pass: two matmuls, not one

Backprop through a linear layer costs two matrix multiplications — this is the single most important fact for reasoning about training cost.

∇W = XT · ∇Yweight gradient · (Cin×B)·(B×Cout)
∇X = ∇Y · WTdata gradient · (B×Cout)·(Cout×Cin)
Why this matters

The weight gradient ∇W needs the saved input X; the data gradient ∇X propagates the error to the previous layer and needs W. Because ∇W consumes X, the forward activations of every layer must be kept alive until the backward pass reaches them — this is the root cause of activation memory blowup (§4).

The activation gradient also flows back through the nonlinearity (e.g. dReLU/dx gates ∇Y), and then the optimizer applies the update:

W′ = W − η · ∇WSGD weight update

The training loop in three calls

  • forward() — run input up to the loss, caching activations.
  • loss.backward() — propagate gradients down, layer by layer.
  • optimizer.step() — apply a weight update at every layer.
Two axes of efficiency

The lecture splits training efficiency into efficient computing (do fewer / cheaper operations — §2, §3) and efficient storage (hold less in memory — §4). Keep them separate; a method can help one and hurt the other.

02

Efficient computing: sampling the data and the network

Four dimensions reduce the compute of training: training-data sampling, parameter sampling, pruning during training, and quantization during training. This section covers the first two; §3 covers the rest.

Training-data sampling Select-via-Proxy

Not every sample is worth a full gradient step. To pick a budget of b samples, work in K rounds, adding b/K points per round:

  • Train the target model on the current b/K subset.
  • Evaluate the model on the remaining pool.
  • Append the batch with the least confidence score (the hardest / most informative points) to the training set. Repeat.

In the toy example, Round 1 scores four samples (0.99, 0.11, 0.27, 0.97); the two the model is already confident about (x₁, x₄) are dropped, and Round 2 keeps only the uncertain x₂, x₃. Coleman et al. 2019.

Parameter sampling — skip work stochastically

E2-Train attacks training energy from three levels at once: Data-level (SMD), Model-level (SLU), and Algorithm-level (PSG).

Stochastic Mini-batch Dropping (SMD)

Simply skip every mini-batch with probability 0.5 by default. On easy datasets this has negligible impact on final accuracy while roughly halving the batches processed — Wang et al. 2019 report over 80% energy savings.

Dynamically skipping layers SkipNet

Instead of a fixed network, attach a lightweight gating function to each residual block that decides, per input, whether to execute or skip it:

Gi(xi) ∈ {0, 1}gate for layer i
  • The gates G and the residual blocks are trained together.
  • The training objective adds a compute penalty: Loss = acc_loss + computation_loss.
  • Result: the network adapts its depth per input — easy images take a short path, hard ones the full stack. Wang et al. (SkipNet) 2018.
03

Pruning and quantization during training

The other two computing dimensions change the network itself as it trains: zero out weights early (pruning) or shrink the numeric precision of the whole pipeline (quantization).

Finding the winning ticket IMP + rewind

The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis says a dense net hides a small trainable subnetwork. Iterative Magnitude Pruning finds it:

  • Initialize the DNN with random weights w₀.
  • While the sparsity target is not met: train k epochs to convergence → prune p% of the nonzero weights → rewind survivors back to their w₀ values and finetune k more epochs.
  • For larger networks, rewinding to an early checkpoint wᵢ (small i) works better than all the way to w₀. Frankle et al. 2019.
Pruning as architecture search · Liu et al. 2018

"Rethinking the Value of Network Pruning" argues the discovered architecture, not the inherited "important" weights, is what drives final accuracy — so pruning can be read as a form of neural architecture search.

Early-bird tickets stop early

Winning tickets can be drawn very early in training with cheap schemes (early stopping, low-precision, large learning rates). The algorithm tracks the mask distance between successive pruned subnetworks in a FIFO queue; when Max(Q) < ε the mask pattern has stabilized, so training stops and returns the ticket. You et al. 2019 find EB tickets after only 6–20% of training.

Pruning the gradient itself · SDGP

Structured Data Gradient Pruning applies 2:4 sparsity to the data gradient dL/dO · Wᵀ = dL/dI during backprop — accelerating training, not just inference. McDanel et al. 2022.

Low-precision training DoReFa-Net

DoReFa-Net trains with low-bitwidth weights (W), activations (A), and gradients (G):

  • Linearly quantize the weights and activations.
  • Apply stochastic quantization to the gradients — gradients need far more bitwidth than weights or activations, so a deterministic round would introduce bias.

Why stochastic quantization

Suppose you must quantize a = 0.2 to the grid {0, 1}. Deterministic linear quantization gives q(a) = 0 — always — introducing a systematic bias. Stochastic quantization instead rounds probabilistically so it is unbiased in expectation:

q(a) = 1 with p = 0.2 , 0 with p = 0.8𝔼[q(a)] = 0.2 = a

Hybrid Block Floating Point BFP

Standard FP stores an exponent per element; Block Floating Point shares one exponent per tensor (tile) and keeps only the mantissas per element — far cheaper in hardware with comparable representational power.

  • Use BFP for all dot-product operations (convolutions, matmuls, outer products); keep FP for activations, regularizers, etc.
  • Store weights with wider mantissas to avoid data loss over a long training run.
  • ResNet-50 / ImageNet, 90 epochs: 8-bit mantissa + 16-bit weight (hbfp8_16) matches FP32; a 12-bit mantissa does even better. Tile size 24. Drumond et al. 2018.
Two copies of the weights

Forward and gradient computation run in BFP, but the weight update runs in FP (e.g. FP32). So two copies live in memory: a low-precision copy for matmuls and a high-precision master copy that accumulates tiny updates. The quantizer Q bridges them each step.

Because the compute matmuls dominate cost, quantizing them buys most of the speedup; keeping the master weights in FP preserves accuracy. Neural gradients are near-lognormal — one can fit a lognormal to the gradient distribution to pick the optimal bitwidth and quantization interval. Chmiel et al. 2020.

04

Efficient storage: taming activation memory

Training memory is dominated not by weights but by activations — the tensors cached in the forward pass so the backward pass can compute ∇W (recall §1).

Where the memory goes

  • The footprint grows with layer depth: early-layer activations must be held for a long time, all the way until the backward pass reaches them.
  • Activations consume ~13× more memory than weights on average (batch size 48). VGG-16 stores 13.4× the ResNet-18 activation baseline; weights are a thin sliver of each bar.
The asymmetry

Because activations dwarf weights during training, reducing parameter count (pruning, quantization) barely dents peak training memory. The lever that matters is not holding all activations at once.

Block-wise local learning BIM

Conventional masked-image modeling does one global forward then one global backward, so every block's activations must coexist in memory. BIM introduces a local loss per block:

  • Each encoder block i is paired with a decoder block i and trained with its own local objective.
  • Once the parameter updates for block i finish, all buffered intermediate features except xᵢ can be cleared from memory and reused.
  • Net effect: peak memory tracks a single block rather than the whole network's forward pass. Luo et al. 2023.
05

Parameter-efficient finetuning (PEFT)

Large models have billions of parameters; fully finetuning one per downstream task is wasteful. PEFT updates only a tiny slice of weights and still reaches near-full-finetune accuracy. Three canonical methods: Adapter, BitFit, LoRA.

Adapter bottleneck module

Insert a small trainable module twice into each transformer layer (after attention, after the feed-forward). Each adapter is a bottleneck — down-project → nonlinearity → up-project — with a skip connection.

  • Only the adapters are trained; the backbone is frozen.
  • Learnable parameters are just 0.5–8% of the original model. Houlsby et al. 2019.

BitFit bias-only

Even cheaper: freeze everything and finetune only the bias terms (in Q/K/V, the MLP, and layer norms). On BERT this is competitive with — sometimes better than — full finetuning, while touching only 0.09% of parameters. Zaken et al. 2021.

Why bias finetuning is cheap · TinyTL insight

The bias gradient is just a sum of the output gradient: dL/dβ = Σy∈Y dL/dy. It does not depend on the input activations, so updating the bias requires no buffering of intermediate results during the forward pass — a large activation-memory saving on-device. Cai et al. (TinyTL) 2020.

Low-Rank Adaptation LoRA

Freeze the pretrained weight W₀ and learn a low-rank update parameterized as a product of two thin matrices:

h = W₀x + ΔWx = W₀x + BAxonly A, B are trained
  • For a weight of size E×E, take A as E×r and B as r×E, with r ≪ E (e.g. r = 4). LoRA is usually inserted into the attention projections Q/K/V.
  • At inference BA can be merged into W₀ → zero extra compute or storage cost, unlike Adapter.
The compute saving

Finetuning the full Q/K/V costs 3BLE² per block. LoRA replaces that with a rank-r path costing only 6BLrE — since r ≪ E, the second is dramatically smaller. You never form the full E×E gradient.

06

Speculative decoding

Autoregressive decoding is serial: N tokens cost N forward passes. A large model is accurate but slow (Ttot = N·T₁); a small one is fast but inaccurate (N·T₂). Speculative decoding gets the large model's quality at closer to the small model's speed — losslessly.

Draft, then verify

  • A cheap draft model autoregressively proposes several tokens ahead (cost N·T₂).
  • The target model verifies the whole draft in a single parallel forward pass (cost Tval), accepting the longest correct prefix.
Ttot = N·T₂ + Tval < N·T₁speculative < autoregressive
  • If a draft token is wrong, the target supplies the correct token, and the draft resumes from there — output is identical to running the target alone (lossless). Leviathan et al. 2023.
  • Caveat: if too few draft tokens pass verification, speculative decoding can be slower than the plain autoregressive baseline. Acceptance rate is everything.

Variants

Self-Speculative

The draft is a subnetwork of the target (early-exit layers). Intermediate results are reusable; only a small classification head is trained. Zhang 2023 · LayerSkip 2024

SpecInfer

Tree-based speculation: propose a tree of candidate continuations and verify them jointly, raising the accepted-token count per step. Miao 2023

PEARL

Parallel spec-decoding with pre-verify + post-verify for adaptive draft length; the draft keeps decoding during verification. On failure the window shrinks to 1. Liu 2024

DREAM

Multimodal spec-decoding: the target streams intermediate features to the draft to guide it, and prunes redundant visual tokens to cut draft latency. Hu 2025

Training the draft for a better acceptance rate

The draft model can be finetuned on the target's data with a cross-entropy loss ℒCE — often using LoRA on its Q/K/V — so its distribution better matches the target and more tokens pass verification. This closes the loop with §5.

The unifying idea

An ideal draft model needs a high acceptance rate and low execution latency — which is exactly the goal of DNN pruning, quantization, knowledge distillation, and dynamic computing. Speculative decoding turns every model-compression technique in this course into a latency win at inference.

Exam recap

Backward = 2 matmuls

∇W = Xᵀ·∇Y (needs saved X), ∇X = ∇Y·Wᵀ. Caching X for ∇W is why activations dominate memory.

Two efficiency axes

Efficient computing (data sampling, parameter sampling, pruning, quantization) vs efficient storage (activation memory).

Data / parameter sampling

Select-via-proxy keeps least-confident samples. E2-Train drops mini-batches (p=0.5); SkipNet gates layers per input.

Lottery tickets

IMP: train → prune p% → rewind survivors to w₀ (or early wᵢ). Early-bird tickets: stop once mask distance stabilizes.

Low-precision training

DoReFa quantizes W/A linearly, gradients stochastically (unbiased: q(0.2)=1 w.p. 0.2). Gradients need the most bits.

BFP + two copies

One exponent per tile. Matmuls in BFP, weight update in FP — a low-precision copy plus an FP master copy.

PEFT trio

Adapter (0.5–8%, bottleneck) · BitFit (0.09%, bias-only, no activation buffering) · LoRA (h=W₀x+BAx, r≪E, mergeable).

Speculative decoding

Draft proposes, target verifies in one pass: T=NT₂+T_val<NT₁, lossless. Low acceptance can be slower than baseline.
